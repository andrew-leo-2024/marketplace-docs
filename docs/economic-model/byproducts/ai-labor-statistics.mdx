# AI Labor Statistics

## What It Is

AI Labor Statistics is the byproduct of tracking every AI task execution on the platform as a unit of labor: what work was done, how long it took, what it cost, and what a human would have charged for the equivalent output. Over thousands of organizations and millions of task executions, this creates the first empirical dataset of AI labor economics -- the actual cost, speed, and quality of AI performing specific business tasks compared to human labor.

This dataset answers questions that no existing data source can: "How much does it cost for AI to process an insurance claim vs. a human claims adjuster?" or "What is the error rate of AI-generated regulatory filings vs. human-prepared filings?" or "How is the cost of AI contract review declining month-over-month?" These are questions that economists, labor policymakers, workforce planners, and corporate strategists need answered, and the platform generates this data as a natural byproduct of operation.

## How It's Generated

Every task executed on the platform generates labor economics data:

- Task type classification: what business function the task maps to (accounting, legal review, compliance, etc.)
- Execution metrics: time to complete, compute cost, accuracy score, confidence level
- Human equivalence: estimated human hours and cost for the same task (calibrated against industry benchmarks)
- Quality comparison: AI output quality vs. human output quality for validated task types
- Trend data: how AI task costs and quality change over time per task type
- Substitution patterns: which human roles are being augmented vs. replaced and at what rate

## Compounding Effect

AI Labor Statistics compound as the platform accumulates longitudinal data across economic cycles. Year one provides point-in-time comparisons. Year two reveals trends. Year three enables forecasting. By year five, the platform has the most comprehensive AI labor economics dataset in existence, with enough history to model displacement curves, skill transition paths, and economic impact projections. This data becomes essential infrastructure for labor policy, comparable to how BLS data underpins economic policy today.
