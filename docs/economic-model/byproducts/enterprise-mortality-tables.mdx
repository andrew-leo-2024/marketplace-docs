# Enterprise Mortality Tables

## What It Is

Enterprise Mortality Tables is the byproduct of observing organizational lifecycle events across the platform's entire customer base: which organizations scale, which stagnate, which contract, and which die -- correlated with their AI operational patterns, governance maturity, service layer adoption, and decision-making behavior. Just as actuarial mortality tables predict human lifespan based on demographic and health variables, Enterprise Mortality Tables predict organizational lifespan and health based on operational telemetry.

This is the deepest Kitchen byproduct because it synthesizes data from every other byproduct into a single predictive model. Failure Intelligence, Drift Early-Warning, AI Labor Statistics, Economic Shock Absorption, Governance Pattern Libraries, and Human Accountability Infrastructure all feed into the mortality model. An organization's "mortality risk" is a composite of its failure rate, governance maturity, compliance posture, economic resilience, accountability structure, and operational efficiency. No other data source combines these signals because no other platform observes all of them simultaneously.

## How It's Generated

Enterprise Mortality Tables are constructed from the convergence of all platform telemetry:

- Lifecycle events: customer onboarding, scaling, contracting, churning, or failing
- Operational health indicators: usage patterns, failure rates, governance maturity, compliance gaps
- Leading indicators: which behavioral patterns precede organizational stress (reduced usage, service layer detachment, governance degradation)
- Sector-adjusted baselines: what "normal" looks like for an organization of a given size, in a given industry, at a given maturity stage
- Intervention effectiveness: which corrective actions (governance upgrades, compliance remediation) improve survival probability
- Cohort analysis: how organizations that started at the same time diverge over 12-36 months

## Compounding Effect

Enterprise Mortality Tables require years of longitudinal data to become actuarially sound. Year one provides baseline organizational profiles. Year two reveals which profiles correlate with stress. Year three provides the first mortality curves -- the probability that an organization with a given profile will fail within a given timeframe. By year five, the tables have enough history to be actuarially reliable, with cohort data spanning multiple economic cycles.

This is the ultimate Kitchen moat. A competitor cannot build Enterprise Mortality Tables without observing thousands of organizations over multiple years through economic cycles, regulatory changes, and technology transitions. The data is time-locked: it can only be generated by operating at scale through real time. There is no shortcut, no synthetic substitute, and no way to purchase this data on the open market.
